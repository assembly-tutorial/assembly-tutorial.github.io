When we talk about Assembly, we're referring to Assembly Language. It's important to remember that an Assembler is a program that converts human readable instructions into operation codes (opcodes).
I think we can all agree that


global    _start

section   .text
_start:
  mov       rax, 1
  mov       rdi, 1
  mov       rsi, message
  mov       rdx, 13
  syscall
  mov       rax, 60
  xor       rdi, rdi
  syscall

section   .data
message:  db        "Hello, World", 10


Much more readable than

b8 01 00 00 00
bf 01 00 00 00
48 be 00 20 40 00 00 00 00 00
ba 0d 00 00 00
0f 05
b8 3c 00 00 00
48 31 ff
0f 05


Yeah, that's how opcodes looks likes, just a bunch of numbers.

Since each architecture has its own unique set of opcodes, we unfortunately don’t have a universal assembly language. At the same time, there are plenty of assemblers out there, each with its own syntax. In this course, we’ll be focusing on the NASM assembler for Linux, specifically with the x86-64 architecture.
