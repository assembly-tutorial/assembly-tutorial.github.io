As I mentioned in first chapter, each CPU core have registers to store data.


                                         General Purpose Registers
+---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------+
|  Name  |                                       Purpose                                                  |
|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|  RAX   | Accumulator. Results of arithmetic operations are often stored in this register                |
|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|  RBX   | Base. It is generally used for storing the addresses refrence of a memory                      |
|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|  RCX   | Counter. Often used in counting operations such as loops, etc                                  |
|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|  RDX   | Data. It often holds data for arithmetic operations, particularly multiplication and division  |
|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|  RSI   | Source Index. It is frequently used as the source operand for string operations                |
|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|  RDI   | Destination Index. Similar to RSI, it is used as the destination operand for string operations |
|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|  RSP   | Stack pointer. It points to the top of the stack                                               |
|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|  RBP   | The Base pointer. Points to the base of the current function's stack frame                     |
|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| R8–R15 | 64-bit general-purpose registers. They were introduced in the 64-bit systems                   |
+---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------+


All this registers holds 64 bit numbers, but we can also get access to lower bits instead of accessing to all bits each time.

     64     |     32     |     16     |     8
------------|------------|------------|----------
    RAX     |    EAX     |     AX     |   AH/AL 
------------|------------|------------|----------
    RBX     |    EBX     |     BX     |   BH/BL
------------|------------|------------|----------
    RCX     |    ECX     |     CX     |   CH/CL
------------|------------|------------|----------
    RDX     |    EDX     |     DX     |   DH/DL
------------|------------|------------|----------
    RSI     |    ESI     |     SI     |    SIL
------------|------------|------------|----------
    RDI     |    EDI     |     DI     |    DIL
------------|------------|------------|----------
    RBP     |    EBP     |     BP     |    BPL
------------|------------|------------|----------
    RSP     |    ESP     |     SP     |    SPL
------------|------------|------------|----------
   R8-R15   |  R8D-R15D  |  R8W-R15W  |  R8B-R15B


When you look at RAX, RBX, RCX, and RDX, you'll notice they each have two 8-bit partial registers: one for the higher bits and one for the lower bits.
The higher register gives us access to the upper 8 bits of a 16-bit partial register, while the lower register, (surprise surprise), deals with the lower bits.
Other registers gives us access only to lower 8 bits.
These partial registers are great for optimizing both the size and speed of your programs, so make sure to take advantage of them whenever you can.

IMPORTANT: When you accessing to partial registers, higher bits will be cleaned.

Also we have RIP, Instruction Pointer, which is where the operating system keeps track of the next instruction address. There's a lot more to it, but for now, let's keep things straightforward and focus on the basics.
